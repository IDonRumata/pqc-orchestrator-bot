DORA REGULATION - KEY PROVISIONS FOR PQC CYBERSECURITY STARTUPS
Regulation (EU) 2022/2554 of the European Parliament and of the Council
of 14 December 2022 on digital operational resilience for the financial sector
(DORA - Digital Operational Resilience Act)
Official Journal: OJ L 333, 27.12.2022, p. 1-79
Application date: 17 January 2025 (directly applicable in all EU Member States, no transposition needed)
Implementing Acts deadline: ESAs issued technical standards throughout 2024

=== SCOPE AND APPLICABILITY ===

Article 2 - Scope
DORA applies directly to financial entities:
- Credit institutions (banks)
- Payment institutions and e-money institutions
- Investment firms and fund managers (AIFMs, UCITS)
- Insurance and reinsurance undertakings
- Insurance intermediaries
- Crypto-asset service providers (CASPs - relevant for Web3/DeFi)
- Crowdfunding service providers
- Trade repositories, central counterparties, trading venues
- Credit rating agencies, audit firms, data reporting service providers

AND to ICT third-party service providers (ICT TPPs) that provide services to financial entities:
- Cloud service providers
- Data analytics providers
- Data centers
- Software providers (including cybersecurity software vendors)

CRITICAL: ICT third-party providers servicing financial sector are directly regulated under DORA.
A PQC audit platform providing services to banks, insurers or investment firms is an ICT TPP
and must comply with Article 28-30 requirements.

=== ICT RISK MANAGEMENT (CHAPTER II, ARTICLES 5-16) ===

Article 5 - ICT governance and strategy
Management body must:
- Maintain active role in ICT risk management strategy
- Define risk appetite for ICT risk
- Approve ICT investment allocations
- Ensure oversight of major ICT incidents

Article 6 - ICT risk management framework
Financial entities must:
- Maintain a sound, comprehensive and well-documented ICT risk management framework
- Continuously identify, classify and document ICT-supported business functions
- Conduct ICT risk assessments
- Implement protection measures

Article 9 - Protection measures (CRYPTOGRAPHY PROVISIONS)
Under protection requirements:
(b) Policies and procedures for data protection including cryptography and data encryption
(c) Where appropriate, dedicated and isolated backup ICT systems
(f) Controls to prevent unauthorized access to cryptographic keys

CRYPTOGRAPHY SPECIFIC:
- Entities must implement state-of-the-art cryptography appropriate to data sensitivity
- Must document cryptographic policies including key management
- Must plan for cryptographic agility (ability to change algorithms without major disruption)
- PQC readiness: DORA technical standards reference ENISA guidelines on quantum-safe cryptography

Article 13 - Learning and evolving
Financial entities must maintain ICT security awareness programs and training.
Post-quantum cryptography awareness must be included as the threat becomes material.

=== ICT-RELATED INCIDENT MANAGEMENT (CHAPTER III, ARTICLES 17-23) ===

Article 17 - ICT-related incident management
Financial entities must define and implement ICT-related incident management processes:
- Classify and document ICT-related incidents
- Detect, manage and notify significant ICT-related incidents

Article 18 - Classification
Significant incident criteria include:
- Number of clients affected
- Duration of the incident
- Geographic spread
- Data losses and integrity issues
- Criticality of services affected
- Economic impact

Article 19 - Reporting to competent authorities:
- Initial notification: within 4 hours for significant incidents (major incidents: 2 hours)
- Intermediate report: within 72 hours
- Final report: within 1 month

=== DIGITAL OPERATIONAL RESILIENCE TESTING (CHAPTER IV, ARTICLES 24-27) ===

Article 24 - General digital operational resilience testing
All financial entities must maintain and review a digital operational resilience testing program including:
- Vulnerability assessments and scans
- Open source analyses
- Network security assessments
- Gap analyses
- Physical security reviews
- Questionnaires and scanning software solutions
- Source code reviews where feasible
- Scenario-based tests
- Compatibility testing
- Performance testing
- End-to-end testing
- Penetration testing

Article 25 - Advanced testing (TLPT - Threat-Led Penetration Testing)
Significant financial institutions must conduct TLPT at least every 3 years.
Cryptographic testing must include:
- Testing of key management procedures
- Encryption implementation review
- Post-quantum readiness assessment (as per ESA guidelines)

=== ICT THIRD-PARTY RISK MANAGEMENT (CHAPTER V, ARTICLES 28-44) ===

Article 28 - General principles
Financial entities must manage ICT third-party risk with a rigorous internal framework:
- Maintain register of ICT TPP contracts
- Conduct pre-contractual due diligence
- Include mandatory contractual provisions

Article 30 - Key contractual provisions (mandatory for ICT TPP contracts):
(a) Full description of all functions, services and data to be provided
(b) The locations (including data processing locations and regions)
(c) Provisions on availability, authenticity, integrity and confidentiality of data
(d) Description of all subcontracting chains
(e) Audit rights for the financial entity and competent authority

CRITICAL for PQC audit vendors:
(g) Incident-related service levels
(h) Obligations for ICT TPP to assist financial entity with supervisory reviews
(i) Right to terminate in cases of significant changes or security events
(l) Information security requirements including cryptographic standards

Article 31 - Critical ICT third-party service providers (CTPPs)
The ESAs may designate certain ICT TPPs as "critical" - these face EU-level oversight:
- Direct oversight by Lead Overseer (EBA, EIOPA, ESMA)
- Mandatory audits
- Binding recommendations

Major cloud providers and large cybersecurity vendors are likely CTPP candidates.
PQC audit startups below materiality threshold are not CTPP but must meet contract requirements.

=== INFORMATION AND INTELLIGENCE SHARING (ARTICLE 45) ===

Article 45 - Information sharing arrangements
Financial entities may arrange and participate in threat intelligence sharing arrangements.
This is explicitly encouraged and exempt from liability for sharing cyber threat information.

=== RELEVANCE TO PQC AUDIT PLATFORM ===

Position of a PQC audit startup under DORA:

As ICT third-party provider to financial entities:
1. Must implement strong ICT risk management framework (Articles 5-16)
2. Cryptographic standards: must use state-of-the-art and document PQC migration plan
3. Incident management: must maintain and communicate incident response capabilities
4. Contract requirements: must accept Article 30 provisions in client contracts
5. Audit rights: must allow client financial entities to audit the startup's security

As provider of DORA compliance audit services:
1. Must deeply understand DORA requirements for financial entities
2. DORA audit scope includes: ICT risk management, incident management, resilience testing,
   third-party risk management, cryptographic policies
3. PQC gap analysis is explicitly within DORA audit scope:
   - Are cryptographic algorithms state-of-the-art?
   - Is there a documented plan for post-quantum migration?
   - Is there cryptographic agility built into systems?
   - Are key management procedures quantum-resistant?

Key DORA audit deliverables for PQC vendor:
- Cryptographic inventory (algorithms, protocols, key sizes, lifecycle)
- HNDL risk assessment for long-lived sensitive financial data
- PQC migration roadmap aligned with ENISA guidelines
- Testing protocol for cryptographic controls (Article 24-25)
- Third-party cryptographic risk assessment (supply chain PQC)
- Board-ready report for management body (Article 5 governance requirement)

=== IMPLEMENTING TECHNICAL STANDARDS (RTS/ITS) ===

ESAs (EBA, EIOPA, ESMA) issued multiple Technical Standards under DORA:
- RTS on ICT Risk Management (EBA/RTS/2024/08)
- RTS on criteria for critical ICT TPP designation
- RTS on information register
- ITS on incident reporting formats
- RTS on TLPT requirements

Key cryptography requirements from RTS:
- Entities must maintain cryptographic key management policies
- Encryption must use current NIST/ENISA recommended algorithms
- RTS explicitly references need to monitor NIST PQC standardization
- Financial entities in sensitive data categories should begin quantum risk assessment

=== POLISH FINANCIAL SUPERVISION AUTHORITY (KNF) IMPLEMENTATION ===

Poland transposed DORA through Polish Financial Instruments Trading Act amendments.
KNF (Komisja Nadzoru Finansowego) is the competent authority for DORA in Poland:
- Website: https://www.knf.gov.pl
- Polish entities must report DORA-significant incidents to KNF
- KNF actively monitors cybersecurity maturity of Polish financial sector

Polish banking sector DORA readiness:
- NBP (National Bank of Poland) coordinates systemic risk assessments
- Polish banks with EU cross-border operations face dual supervision
- Smaller Polish fintechs may use DORA simplified ICT risk management (Article 16)
