NIS2 DIRECTIVE - KEY PROVISIONS FOR PQC CYBERSECURITY STARTUPS
Directive (EU) 2022/2555 of the European Parliament and of the Council
of 14 December 2022 on measures for a high common level of cybersecurity across the Union
(Official Journal of the EU: OJ L 333, 27.12.2022, p. 80-152)
Repeals and replaces: Directive 2016/1148 (NIS1)
Transposition deadline: 17 October 2024 (Member States must implement into national law)

=== SCOPE AND APPLICABILITY ===

Article 2 - Scope
NIS2 applies to medium and large entities operating in sectors listed in Annexes I and II,
AND to all entities regardless of size in sectors designated critical infrastructure.

Annex I - Essential sectors (high criticality):
- Energy (electricity, gas, oil, heating, cooling, hydrogen)
- Transport (air, rail, water, road)
- Banking and financial market infrastructure
- Health sector
- Drinking water and wastewater
- Digital infrastructure: IXPs, DNS, TLD, cloud computing, data centers, CDN, trust services,
  electronic communications networks and services
- ICT service management (B2B): managed service providers (MSPs) and managed security
  service providers (MSSPs) - DIRECTLY RELEVANT to PQC audit platform
- Space (ground-based infrastructure)
- Public administration

Annex II - Other important sectors:
- Postal and courier services
- Waste management
- Manufacture/production/distribution of chemicals
- Food production, processing and distribution
- Manufacturing (medical devices, electronics, machinery, vehicles)
- Digital providers: online marketplaces, search engines, social networks
- Research organizations

Size thresholds (Article 2):
- Medium entity: 50+ employees OR EUR 10M+ annual turnover/balance sheet
- Large entity: 250+ employees OR EUR 50M+ annual turnover OR EUR 43M+ balance sheet
- Micro and small entities are generally exempt UNLESS they are key infrastructure providers,
  trust service providers, qualified trust service providers, TLD registries, DNS providers,
  sole providers in Member States, or their disruption would significantly impact public safety.

=== SECURITY REQUIREMENTS (ARTICLE 21) ===

Article 21 - Cybersecurity risk-management measures (CRITICAL for PQC audit services)

Essential and important entities must take appropriate technical, operational and
organizational measures to manage risks posed to the security of network and information
systems. Measures must be proportionate to the risk, taking into account:
- state of the art and EU standards (ENISA guidelines, ISO/IEC, NIST)
- costs of implementation
- likelihood of incidents and their severity

Mandatory minimum measures (Article 21(2)):
(a) Policies on risk analysis and information system security
(b) Incident handling (prevention, detection, response and recovery)
(c) Business continuity management and crisis management
(d) Supply chain security including relationships with direct suppliers and service providers
(e) Security in network and information systems acquisition, development and maintenance,
    including vulnerability handling and disclosure
(f) Policies and procedures to assess effectiveness of cybersecurity risk-management measures
(g) Basic cyber hygiene practices and cybersecurity training
(h) Policies and procedures regarding use of cryptography and, where appropriate,
    ENCRYPTION - directly invokes cryptographic standards including PQC migration
(i) Human resources security, access control policies and asset management
(j) Use of multi-factor authentication or continuous authentication solutions,
    secured voice/video/text communications
(k) Secured emergency communication systems within the entity

SPECIFIC ON CRYPTOGRAPHY (Article 21(2)(h)):
The Directive explicitly requires cryptography policies. ENISA guidelines (issued under NIS2)
specifically recommend migrating to quantum-resistant cryptography given HNDL threats.
Entities subject to NIS2 that process sensitive data must document their cryptographic
posture and roadmap for post-quantum migration.

=== INCIDENT REPORTING OBLIGATIONS (ARTICLES 23-24) ===

Article 23 - Reporting obligations:
1. Early warning: within 24 hours of becoming aware of significant incident
2. Incident notification: within 72 hours with initial assessment of severity and impact
3. Intermediate report (if requested by CSIRT or authority)
4. Final report: within 1 month containing full description, type of threat, cause,
   applied mitigation measures, cross-border impact

Significant incident criteria:
- Caused or capable of causing severe operational disruption or financial loss
- Caused or capable of causing considerable material or non-material damage

=== SUPERVISION AND ENFORCEMENT (ARTICLES 32-36) ===

Article 32-33 - Supervisory regime:
- Essential entities: ex-ante and ex-post supervision (proactive)
- Important entities: ex-post supervision (reactive, after incident or complaint)

Article 34 - Supervisory measures:
- Targeted security audits based on risk assessments
- On-site and off-site inspections
- Security scans using non-intrusive technical means
- Requests for information and evidence of implementation
- Access to data and documentation

Article 36 - Administrative penalties:
- Essential entities: up to EUR 10 000 000 or at least 2% of global annual turnover (whichever is higher)
- Important entities: up to EUR 7 000 000 or at least 1.4% of global annual turnover (whichever is higher)

Article 20 - Governance:
Management bodies of essential and important entities must:
- Approve cybersecurity risk-management measures
- Oversee their implementation
- Attend cybersecurity training
- Be liable for infringements

=== SUPPLY CHAIN SECURITY (ARTICLE 22) ===

Article 22 - Union-level coordinated security risk assessment of ICT supply chains:
ENISA, in cooperation with the Cooperation Group, conducts coordinated risk assessments
for critical ICT supply chains. This directly applies to cryptographic library vendors
and PQC implementation providers.

Supply chain cybersecurity requirements for essential entities include:
- Assessing security posture of suppliers and service providers
- Overall quality of products and security practices, including secure development processes
- Presence of vulnerabilities and security patches

=== NATIONAL CYBERSECURITY STRATEGY AND CSIRT ===

Article 7 - National cybersecurity strategy requirements
Article 10 - CSIRTs (Computer Security Incident Response Teams):
Each Member State must designate one or more CSIRTs for essential and important sectors.

For Poland: CERT Polska (NASK) and ABW are the primary CSIRTs.
For EU coordination: ENISA coordinates the EU-CyCLONe (Article 16) for large-scale incidents.

=== ENISA GUIDELINES UNDER NIS2 ===

Under Article 24, ENISA issues guidelines on:
- Technical requirements, methodologies and simplified processes for cybersecurity measures
- Binding technical implementation measures (for DNS providers, TLD registries, CSPs, MSSPs)
- PQC migration guidelines (ENISA has published dedicated PQC guidance documents)

ENISA PQC Recommendation:
- Entities should inventory current cryptographic assets (cryptographic agility)
- Plan for hybrid approaches (classic + PQC) as transition strategy
- Prioritize ML-KEM for key exchange (FIPS 203) and ML-DSA for signatures (FIPS 204)
- Timeline: ENISA recommends organizations begin migration planning by 2025-2026,
  complete for critical systems by 2030

=== RELEVANCE TO PQC AUDIT PLATFORM ===

A PQC audit service provider (MSSP/MSP category) is directly subject to NIS2 if:
- Provides managed security services to essential or important entities
- Size threshold: medium (50+ employees) or large (250+ employees)
- If below threshold but sole or dominant provider in a Member State: still covered

Even for non-covered startups, offering NIS2 compliance audits requires expertise in:
- Article 21 implementation assessments
- Cryptography policy reviews (Article 21(2)(h))
- Supply chain security assessments (Article 22)
- Incident response capabilities
- Management governance requirements (Article 20)

PQC audit scope derived from NIS2:
1. Audit current cryptographic inventory (algorithms, key sizes, protocols)
2. Identify NIS2-mandated cryptography requirements (TLS versions, encryption standards)
3. Map gap to NIST FIPS 203/204/205 compliance
4. Assess HNDL (Harvest Now Decrypt Later) risk for long-term sensitive data
5. Design hybrid migration roadmap
6. Prepare documentation for supervisory authority review
